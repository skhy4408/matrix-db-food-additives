# Research: Preservatives E226-E252

## E226 - Calcium Sulfite

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E226
- **INS Number**: 226
- **CAS Number**: 10257-55-3
- **Common Name**: Calcium Sulfite
- **Scientific Name**: Calcium sulfite
- **Japanese Name**: 亜硫酸カルシウム
- **Japanese Label Name**: 亜硫酸Ca

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: preservative, antioxidant
- **Sub-class**: inorganic
- **Source Type**: synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: sulfite

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E226), restricted use
- **USA**: Approved (GRAS affirmed)
- **Japan**: Approved (指定添加物)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved
- **Canada**: Approved

### Safety Data
- **ADI**: 0-0.7 mg/kg bw/day (as SO2)
- **JECFA Evaluation**: 2016
- **EFSA Evaluation**: 2016

### Common Uses
- Wine preservation
- Dried fruit
- Dehydrated potatoes
- Fruit juices

### Fun Facts
- Part of the sulfite family used for thousands of years in winemaking
- Can trigger allergic reactions in sulfite-sensitive individuals (~1% of population)
- Sulfites must be labeled when present above 10 ppm in many countries

- **Scientific Name**: Potassium hydrogen sulfite

- **Functional Class**: preservative, antioxidant

## E230 - Biphenyl (Diphenyl)

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E230
- **INS Number**: 230
- **CAS Number**: 92-52-4
- **Common Name**: Biphenyl / Diphenyl
- **Scientific Name**: Biphenyl / 1,1'-Biphenyl
- **Japanese Name**: ジフェニル
- **Japanese Label Name**: ジフェニル

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: preservative (surface treatment)
- **Sub-class**: aromatic hydrocarbon
- **Source Type**: synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: phenyl compound

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E230), restricted to citrus fruit peel surface treatment only
- **USA**: Approved for citrus fruit wrapping paper only
- **Japan**: Approved for surface treatment
- **Australia/NZ**: BANNED
- **Canada**: Approved with restrictions

### Safety Data
- **ADI**: 0-0.5 mg/kg bw/day
- **JECFA Evaluation**: 1974
- **EFSA Evaluation**: 2014

### Common Uses
- Citrus fruit peel surface treatment (oranges, lemons, grapefruits)
- Impregnated in fruit wrapping papers and shipping boxes
- NOT allowed in food directly - only surface contact

### Fun Facts
- Literally sprayed or applied to orange and lemon peels to prevent mold during shipping
- Must be washed off before consuming the peel (zest)
- Banned in Australia due to safety concerns
- Not intended to be eaten - remains on outer surface only
- One of the few additives applied EXTERNALLY to food, not mixed in

### Controversy
- Suspected endocrine disruptor
- Potential neurotoxicity concerns
- Australia banned it despite EU/US approval
- Should NEVER peel citrus with teeth if E230 was used

## E231 - Orthophenyl Phenol (OPP)

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E231
- **INS Number**: 231
- **CAS Number**: 90-43-7
- **Common Name**: Orthophenyl Phenol (OPP) / 2-Phenylphenol
- **Scientific Name**: 2-Phenylphenol
- **Japanese Name**: オルトフェニルフェノール
- **Japanese Label Name**: OPP

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: preservative (surface treatment, fungicide)
- **Sub-class**: phenolic compound
- **Source Type**: synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: phenyl compound

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E231), restricted to citrus fruit surface treatment only
- **USA**: Approved as pesticide for citrus fruits
- **Japan**: Approved for surface treatment (農薬としても承認)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved with restrictions
- **Canada**: Approved for surface treatment

### Safety Data
- **ADI**: 0-0.2 mg/kg bw/day
- **JECFA Evaluation**: 2014
- **EFSA Evaluation**: 2014

### Common Uses
- Citrus fruit peel surface treatment (post-harvest fungicide)
- Applied to oranges, lemons, limes, grapefruits
- Prevents mold growth during transport and storage
- Sometimes used in fruit wrapping materials

### Fun Facts
- Works similarly to E230 but is chemically different (has an -OH group)
- Also used as a disinfectant and antiseptic in non-food applications
- Must be declared on labels in many countries
- More effective against certain fungi than E230
- Should be washed off before using citrus zest in cooking

### Controversy
- Classified as possible carcinogen in high doses (animal studies)
- Some countries have stricter limits than others
- Debate over residue levels remaining on peel vs. penetration into fruit flesh

## E234 - Nisin

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E234
- **INS Number**: 234
- **CAS Number**: 1414-45-5
- **Common Name**: Nisin
- **Scientific Name**: Nisin (polycyclic antibacterial peptide)
- **Japanese Name**: ナイシン
- **Japanese Label Name**: ナイシン

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: preservative (antimicrobial peptide, bacteriocin)
- **Sub-class**: natural antimicrobial
- **Source Type**: NATURAL (produced by bacteria)
- **Chemical Group**: lantibiotic peptide

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E234)
- **USA**: Approved (GRAS)
- **Japan**: Approved (既存添加物 - existing food additive)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved
- **Canada**: Approved

### Safety Data
- **ADI**: 0-0.13 mg/kg bw/day (expressed as pure nisin)
- **JECFA Evaluation**: 2016
- **EFSA Evaluation**: 2017

### Common Uses
- Processed cheese and cheese products
- Canned vegetables
- Dairy desserts
- Clotted cream
- Pasteurized liquid eggs
- Beer (to prevent spoilage)

### Fun Facts
- NATURAL preservative produced by "good bacteria" (Lactococcus lactis) during fermentation
- A "bacteriocin" - literally a peptide made by bacteria to kill other bacteria
- Works like a natural antibiotic but is NOT considered an antibiotic
- Discovered in 1928 (same era as penicillin!)
- Named after the Latin "Nisus" (Nissan cheese factory in England where it was discovered)
- Breaks down in stomach acid, so no impact on gut bacteria
- One of the safest preservatives - it's made by the same bacteria used to make cheese

### Special Note
- Considered "clean label" friendly due to natural origin
- Growing popularity as alternative to synthetic preservatives

## E235 - Natamycin (Pimaricin)

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E235
- **INS Number**: 235
- **CAS Number**: 7681-93-8
- **Common Name**: Natamycin / Pimaricin
- **Scientific Name**: Natamycin (polyene macrolide antibiotic)
- **Japanese Name**: ナタマイシン
- **Japanese Label Name**: ナタマイシン

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: preservative (antifungal agent)
- **Sub-class**: natural antimicrobial
- **Source Type**: NATURAL (produced by bacteria)
- **Chemical Group**: polyene macrolide

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E235), restricted to surface treatment of cheese and dried sausages
- **USA**: Approved (GRAS)
- **Japan**: Approved (既存添加物)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved
- **Canada**: Approved

### Safety Data
- **ADI**: 0-0.3 mg/kg bw/day
- **JECFA Evaluation**: 2016
- **EFSA Evaluation**: 2009

### Common Uses
- Cheese surface treatment (prevents mold on cheese rinds)
- Dried and fermented sausage casings
- Surface treatment of baked goods
- Yogurt (in some countries)

### Fun Facts
- NATURAL antifungal produced by soil bacterium Streptomyces natalensis
- Named after Natal, South Africa where the bacteria was discovered
- Works ONLY on the surface - not meant to penetrate into food
- Also called "pimaricin" (from Pietermaritzburg, South Africa)
- Used in medicine as antifungal for eye infections (not taken orally)
- The white coating you see on some cheese rinds is often natamycin
- Extremely effective against yeasts and molds but has NO effect on bacteria
- Does NOT promote antibiotic resistance because it's only used on surfaces and breaks down quickly

### Special Note
- Considered natural and "clean label" friendly
- Popular in organic and natural food products

## E239 - Hexamethylenetetramine (Hexamine)

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E239
- **INS Number**: 239
- **CAS Number**: 100-97-0
- **Common Name**: Hexamethylenetetramine / Hexamine / Urotropin
- **Scientific Name**: Hexamethylenetetramine
- **Japanese Name**: ヘキサメチレンテトラミン
- **Japanese Label Name**: ヘキサミン

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: preservative
- **Sub-class**: organic compound
- **Source Type**: synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: cyclic tertiary amine

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: BANNED for food use (removed from approved list)
- **USA**: BANNED for food use (not approved by FDA)
- **Japan**: Approved with restrictions (特定の食品のみ)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved for provolone cheese only (235)
- **Canada**: Restricted use

### Safety Data
- **ADI**: NOT ESTABLISHED (withdrawn in most regions)
- **JECFA Evaluation**: Withdrawn from evaluation
- **EFSA**: Not approved for food use

### Common Uses (where still legal)
- Provolone cheese (traditional use)
- Caviar (in some countries)
- Marinated herring (limited regions)

### Fun Facts
- BANNED in EU and USA but still used in some countries
- Breaks down into formaldehyde in acidic conditions - major safety concern
- Also used as fuel tablets for camping stoves (same chemical!)
- Historical use dates back to early 1900s
- One of the most controversial preservatives still in use anywhere
- The formaldehyde formed is the reason for the ban in most countries

### Controversy
- **Major Safety Concern**: Decomposes into formaldehyde (a known carcinogen)
- Banned in most developed countries
- Still used in some traditional cheese production where grandfathered in
- No established ADI due to formaldehyde formation
- Example of a preservative being phased out globally

## E242 - Dimethyl Dicarbonate (DMDC)

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E242
- **INS Number**: 242
- **CAS Number**: 4525-33-1
- **Common Name**: Dimethyl Dicarbonate / DMDC / Velcorin
- **Scientific Name**: Dimethyl dicarbonate
- **Japanese Name**: ジメチルジカルボネート
- **Japanese Label Name**: DMDC

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: preservative (cold sterilant)
- **Sub-class**: organic carbonate
- **Source Type**: synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: dicarbonate ester

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E242), restricted use in beverages only
- **USA**: Approved (GRAS) for beverages
- **Japan**: Approved (指定添加物)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved with restrictions
- **Canada**: Approved for beverages

### Safety Data
- **ADI**: NOT SPECIFIED (rapidly hydrolyzes to CO2 and methanol at safe levels)
- **JECFA Evaluation**: 2005
- **EFSA Evaluation**: 2015

### Common Uses
- Wine (still and sparkling)
- Ready-to-drink tea and iced tea
- Sports drinks and isotonic beverages
- Fruit juice-based drinks
- Flavored water

### Common Usage Levels
- Maximum: 250 mg/L in wine; 250 mg/L in ready-to-drink tea

### Fun Facts
- Breaks down within SECONDS into CO2 and trace methanol (both already in beverages naturally)
- Known as "cold sterilization" - kills microbes without heating
- Leaves virtually NO residue in final product (self-eliminating)
- Brand name "Velcorin" is well-known in beverage industry
- Allows preservation of beverages without heat treatment (preserves flavor)
- The methanol produced is less than what's naturally in fruit juices
- Must be added with precise dosing equipment due to rapid reaction

### Special Note
- Considered low-risk because it doesn't persist in the final product
- Unique among preservatives for its self-eliminating property

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E243
- **INS Number**: 243
- **CAS Number**: 60372-77-2
- **Common Name**: Ethyl Lauroyl Arginate / LAE
- **Scientific Name**: Ethyl Nα-lauroyl-L-arginate hydrochloride
- **Japanese Name**: エチルラウロイルアルギネート
- **Japanese Label Name**: LAE

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: preservative (antimicrobial)
- **Sub-class**: cationic surfactant
- **Source Type**: synthetic (from natural components)
- **Chemical Group**: arginine derivative

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E243), added in 2015
- **USA**: Approved (GRAS) since 2005
- **Japan**: Approved (指定添加物)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved
- **Canada**: Approved

### Safety Data
- **ADI**: 0-3 mg/kg bw/day
- **JECFA Evaluation**: 2014
- **EFSA Evaluation**: 2014

### Common Uses
- Processed meat products (ham, sausages, bacon)
- Sauces and dressings (mayonnaise, salsa)
- Egg-based products
- Guacamole and fresh-cut vegetables
- Tortillas and baked goods

### Fun Facts
- One of the NEWEST preservatives approved (EU: 2015)
- Made from amino acid L-arginine and lauric acid (both found in nature)
- Breaks down in the body into natural components (arginine, lauric acid, ethanol)
- Effective at very low concentrations
- Works against both bacteria AND fungi
- Marketed as "clean label" friendly despite being synthetic
- Particularly popular in refrigerated fresh foods
- Brand name "MireNat" in commercial applications

### Special Note
- Represents modern trend toward safer, biodegradable preservatives
- Derived from natural building blocks but synthesized chemically

## E250 - Sodium Nitrite

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E250
- **INS Number**: 250
- **CAS Number**: 7632-00-0
- **Common Name**: Sodium Nitrite
- **Scientific Name**: Sodium nitrite
- **Japanese Name**: 亜硝酸ナトリウム
- **Japanese Label Name**: 亜硝酸Na

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: preservative, color fixative
- **Sub-class**: inorganic
- **Source Type**: synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: nitrite

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E250), strictly regulated
- **USA**: Approved, regulated by USDA and FDA
- **Japan**: Approved (指定添加物), strict limits
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved with restrictions
- **Canada**: Approved with limits

### Safety Data
- **ADI**: 0-0.07 mg/kg bw/day (nitrite ion - group ADI with E249)
- **JECFA Evaluation**: 2017
- **EFSA Evaluation**: 2017

### Common Uses
- Bacon and ham (gives pink/red color)
- Hot dogs and sausages
- Deli meats and cold cuts
- Corned beef
- Salami and other cured meats
- Smoked fish

### Common Usage Levels
- Maximum residual: 50-150 mg/kg depending on product (EU)
- Ingoing amount: typically 120-200 ppm (USA)

### Fun Facts
- THIS is what makes bacon PINK! Without it, bacon would be gray/brown
- Prevents deadly botulism (Clostridium botulinum) - one of the most potent toxins known
- Called "pink salt" or "curing salt" (Prague Powder #1) when mixed with regular salt
- Has been used for meat preservation for thousands of years (before chemistry was understood)
- The pink color is from nitric oxide combining with myoglobin in meat
- Also adds characteristic "cured meat" flavor

### The Nitrite Controversy - Risk vs. Benefit Balance

**THE BAD:**
1. **Nitrosamine Formation**: Can form carcinogenic nitrosamines, especially when:
   - Meat is cooked at high temperatures (frying, grilling)
   - Combined with amines from protein
   - Acidic stomach environment

2. **Cancer Link**: 
   - IARC classified processed meat as Group 1 carcinogen (2015)
   - Nitrites identified as contributing factor
   - Linked to colorectal cancer, stomach cancer

3. **Other Concerns**:
   - Can reduce oxygen-carrying capacity of blood (methemoglobinemia) in infants
   - Headaches in sensitive individuals

**THE GOOD:**
1. **Prevents Botulism**: 
   - Stops Clostridium botulinum growth
   - Botulism is DEADLY (fatality rate up to 10% even with treatment)
   - One of the most powerful natural toxins known

2. **Food Safety**:
   - Prevents other dangerous bacteria (Listeria, Salmonella)
   - Extends shelf life safely
   - Reduces food waste and foodborne illness

**THE DILEMMA:**
- "Nitrite-free" bacon still contains nitrates/nitrites from celery powder or other "natural" sources
- "Uncured" meats often have HIGHER nitrite levels from natural sources
- Complete removal would significantly increase botulism risk
- Modern science is searching for safer alternatives

**CURRENT CONSENSUS:**
- Benefits (preventing acute botulism) outweigh risks (long-term cancer) when used properly
- Consumption should be limited (not every day)
- Vitamin C/E added to products helps block nitrosamine formation
- Research continues on safer alternatives

### Mitigation Strategies in Modern Products
- Add ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) or erythorbate to block nitrosamine formation
- Use minimum effective levels (regulatory limits decreasing over time)
- Some producers experimenting with natural antimicrobials to reduce nitrite dependence

### Special Note
- E250 represents the classic food additive dilemma: known risks vs. known benefits
- Unlike many additives, nitrites serve CRITICAL safety function, not just cosmetic
- Debate continues between food safety and public health communities

## Key Takeaways

1. **Natural Preservatives**: E234 (Nisin) and E235 (Natamycin) are both produced by bacteria and considered natural antimicrobials

2. **Surface Treatments**: E230 and E231 are applied to citrus fruit peels, not mixed into foods

3. **Banned/Restricted**: E239 (Hexamine) is banned in EU/USA due to formaldehyde formation

4. **Newest Addition**: E243 (LAE) approved in EU in 2015, represents modern preservative technology

5. **Biggest Controversy**: E250 (Sodium nitrite) - prevents deadly botulism BUT linked to cancer when forming nitrosamines

6. **Sulfite Family**: E226 and E228 are sulfites - major allergen concern, must be labeled

7. **Self-Eliminating**: E242 (DMDC) breaks down rapidly into CO2 and trace methanol, leaving no residue
