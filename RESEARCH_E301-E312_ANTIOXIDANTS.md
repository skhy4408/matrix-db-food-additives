# Research Data: Antioxidant Additives E301-E312

Research completed: 2026-04-04

## Batch Overview: Antioxidants E301-E319

This batch covers 10 antioxidant food additives:
- E301-E302, E304: Ascorbate derivatives (Vitamin C related)
- E306-E309: Tocopherols (Vitamin E - natural or synthetic)
- E310-E312: Gallates (synthetic antioxidants)

Note: E300 (Ascorbic acid) already exists in the database. E315-E319 skipped for this batch.

## E301 - Sodium Ascorbate

### Basic Identification
- **E Number**: E301
- **INS Number**: 301
- **CAS Number**: 134-03-2
- **Common Name**: Sodium Ascorbate
- **Scientific Name**: Sodium L-ascorbate
- **Japanese Name**: L-アスコルビン酸ナトリウム
- **Japanese Label Name**: ビタミンCナトリウム
- **Aliases**: Sodium vitamin C, Sodium salt of ascorbic acid

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: antioxidant
- **Sub-class**: vitamin derivative
- **Source Type**: synthetic (from ascorbic acid)
- **Chemical Group**: organic acid salt

### Natural Occurrence
- **Found in Nature**: No (derivative of natural vitamin C)
- **Relationship to Nature**: Sodium salt of ascorbic acid (Vitamin C), which is naturally present in citrus fruits, strawberries, kiwi, bell peppers, broccoli, tomatoes
- **Description**: Synthetically produced sodium salt of L-ascorbic acid (Vitamin C). The ascorbic acid portion is identical to naturally occurring vitamin C.

### Manufacturing
- **Method**: Chemical synthesis
- **Description**: Produced by dissolving ascorbic acid in water with addition of sodium bicarbonate. After the reaction is complete and foaming stops, the compound is precipitated with isopropanol, yielding a white or slightly yellowish powder.

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E301), quantum satis in many categories
- **USA**: GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe)
- **Japan**: Approved (既存添加物)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved (301)
- **Canada**: Approved
- **EFSA Last Evaluated**: 2015
- **Special Notes**: Re-evaluated together with E300 and E302 in 2015 EFSA opinion

### Safety Evaluations
- **JECFA ADI**: Not specified (no ADI necessary for ascorbic acid and its salts)
- **JECFA Evaluation Year**: Multiple evaluations
- **EFSA ADI**: Not specified (unnecessary to establish ADI - no health risks at normal intake levels)
- **EFSA Evaluation Year**: 2015
- **Safety Notes**: EFSA concluded no health risks identified at normal intake levels. No serious toxicity concerns from JECFA.

### Commonly Found In
- Cured meats (bacon, ham, pepperoni, sausage, salami, prosciutto)
- Fruit preserves and jams
- Fruit juices and beverages
- Canned fruits and vegetables
- Frozen foods
- Ground meat products
- Cold cuts
- Breakfast cereals (fortified)

### Fun Facts
1. **Color Preservation Hero**: In cured meats, sodium ascorbate preserves the appealing purple-red hue by preventing oxidation of myoglobin, keeping products looking fresh longer.

2. **Curing Accelerator**: Acts as a curing accelerator that speeds up conversion of nitrite to nitric oxide, ensuring faster and more uniform color development in processed meats.

3. **Nitrosamine Fighter**: Significantly reduces formation of nitrosamines (potentially harmful compounds that can form from nitrite under high heat) in cured meats.

4. **Dual Function**: Serves both as an antioxidant to prevent rancidity AND as a nutritional fortification agent providing vitamin C.

5. **Less Acidic Alternative**: More pH-neutral than pure ascorbic acid, making it gentler on stomach and suitable for people who find regular vitamin C supplements too acidic.

### Other Uses
- **Cosmetics**: Antioxidant and skin-brightening agent
- **Pharmaceutical**: Vitamin C supplement (gentler form)
- **Industrial**: Oxygen scavenger in packaging

## E302 - Calcium Ascorbate

### Basic Identification
- **E Number**: E302
- **INS Number**: 302
- **CAS Number**: 5743-27-1
- **Common Name**: Calcium Ascorbate
- **Scientific Name**: Calcium L-ascorbate
- **Japanese Name**: L-アスコルビン酸カルシウム
- **Japanese Label Name**: ビタミンCカルシウム
- **Aliases**: Calcium salt of ascorbic acid, Calcium vitamin C

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: antioxidant, acidity regulator
- **Sub-class**: vitamin derivative
- **Source Type**: synthetic (from ascorbic acid)
- **Chemical Group**: organic acid salt

### Natural Occurrence
- **Found in Nature**: No (derivative of natural vitamin C)
- **Relationship to Nature**: Calcium salt of ascorbic acid (Vitamin C), which is naturally present in many fruits and vegetables
- **Description**: Synthetically produced calcium salt of L-ascorbic acid. Non-acidic, gentle form of vitamin C.

### Manufacturing
- **Method**: Chemical synthesis
- **Description**: Produced by reacting ascorbic acid with calcium carbonate or calcium hydroxide to form the calcium salt. The resulting compound is a white to slightly yellow powder.

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E302), quantum satis in many categories
- **USA**: GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe)
- **Japan**: Approved (既存添加物)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved (302)
- **Canada**: Approved
- **EFSA Last Evaluated**: 2015
- **Special Notes**: Re-evaluated together with E300 and E301 in 2015 EFSA opinion

### Safety Evaluations
- **JECFA ADI**: Not specified (no ADI necessary for ascorbic acid and its salts)
- **EFSA ADI**: Not specified (unnecessary to establish ADI - no safety concerns at typical intake levels)
- **EFSA Evaluation Year**: 2015
- **Safety Notes**: EFSA concluded no adverse effects in animal studies. JECFA confirmed no carcinogenic or mutagenic properties. Excessive consumption can lead to side effects similar to vitamin C overdose (diarrhea, abdominal pain, increased gas, urinary tract irritation, and rarely kidney stones in predisposed individuals).

### Commonly Found In
- Cured or cooked meat products
- Ground meat (prevents discoloration)
- Fruit preserves, jellies, jams, compotes
- Fruit juices and fruit-flavored candies
- Calcium-fortified beverages
- Breakfast cereals (fortified)
- Fresh-cut fruit (dipping solution to prevent browning)
- Cold cuts and uncured sausages

### Fun Facts
1. **Double Nutrient Power**: Provides both vitamin C AND calcium in one compound, making it a dual-purpose nutritional fortification agent.

2. **Color Intensifier**: Intensifies and maintains the vibrant color of cured meats, making products more visually appealing.

3. **Gentle Giant**: Non-acidic form of vitamin C that's easier on the stomach than regular ascorbic acid, ideal for sensitive individuals.

4. **Browning Blocker**: Used as a dipping solution for freshly cut fruits to prevent enzymatic browning, keeping fruit looking fresh and appetizing.

5. **Flavor Enhancer**: Despite being less acidic than ascorbic acid, it can still enhance tart flavor and fresh taste in fruit products like jellies and jams.

### Other Uses
- **Pharmaceutical**: Calcium and vitamin C dietary supplement
- **Cosmetics**: Stabilizer and antioxidant
- **Animal feed**: Nutritional supplement

## E304 - Ascorbyl Palmitate (Fatty Acid Esters of Ascorbic Acid)

### Basic Identification
- **E Number**: E304 (E304i for ascorbyl palmitate, E304ii for ascorbyl stearate)
- **INS Number**: 304
- **CAS Number**: 137-66-6 (ascorbyl palmitate)
- **Common Name**: Ascorbyl Palmitate
- **Scientific Name**: L-Ascorbyl 6-palmitate
- **Japanese Name**: パルミチン酸アスコルビル (L-アスコルビル6-パルミタート)
- **Japanese Label Name**: パルミチン酸アスコルビル
- **Aliases**: 6-Palmitoyl-3-keto-L-gulofuranolactone, Fatty acid esters of ascorbic acid

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: antioxidant
- **Sub-class**: vitamin derivative (fat-soluble)
- **Source Type**: synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: organic ester

### Natural Occurrence
- **Found in Nature**: No (synthetic ester)
- **Relationship to Nature**: Ester formed from naturally occurring ascorbic acid (vitamin C) and palmitic acid (found in palm oil, meat, dairy)
- **Description**: Fat-soluble form of vitamin C created by esterifying ascorbic acid with palmitic acid.

### Manufacturing
- **Method**: Chemical synthesis (esterification)
- **Description**: Produced by reaction of the primary alcohol group of ascorbic acid (vitamin C) with the carboxylic acid group of palmitic acid. Simple monoester with ascorbyl and palmitate moieties in 1:1 mole ratio.
- **Molecular Formula**: C22H38O7

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E304i and E304ii), quantum satis in many categories
- **USA**: GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe)
- **Japan**: Approved (指定添加物 - Designated additive)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved (304)
- **Canada**: Approved
- **EFSA Last Evaluated**: 2015
- **Special Notes**: E304i (ascorbyl palmitate) and E304ii (ascorbyl stearate) evaluated together

### Safety Evaluations
- **JECFA ADI**: 0-25 mg/kg bw/day (for ascorbyl palmitate)
- **EFSA ADI**: 1.1 mg/kg bw/day (2015 re-evaluation)
- **EFSA Evaluation Year**: 2015
- **Safety Notes**: EFSA concluded safe at reported uses and use levels. FDA lists as GRAS for human consumption when used in accordance with good manufacturing practice.

### Commonly Found In
- Vegetable oils (preservation)
- Margarine and shortening
- Fatty snack foods
- Baked goods (biscuits, cereals)
- Frozen foods
- Canned foods
- Infant formula (protects DHA and ARA from oxidation)
- Vitamin supplements (fat-soluble vitamin C)

### Fun Facts
1. **Fat-Soluble Vitamin C**: Unlike regular vitamin C which is water-soluble, ascorbyl palmitate dissolves in fats and oils, allowing it to protect lipid-based products from oxidation.

2. **Dual Identity**: Functions both as a source of vitamin C (when consumed, it breaks down into vitamin C and palmitic acid) AND as a powerful fat-preserving antioxidant.

3. **Margarine Guardian**: FDA allows up to 0.02% by weight in margarine as an optional preservative, significantly extending shelf life by preventing rancidity.

4. **Free Radical Fighter**: Dissolves directly into lipid systems where it neutralizes free radicals and inhibits the oxidation chain reaction at its source - protecting fats from the inside out.

5. **Baby Formula Protector**: Essential in infant formula for protecting vulnerable polyunsaturated fats like DHA and ARA from oxidation, ensuring babies get their vital nutrients intact.

### Other Uses
- **Cosmetics**: Skin-care products (antioxidant, preservative)
- **Pharmaceutical**: Vitamin supplement, tablet stabilizer
- **Animal feed**: Antioxidant preservative

## E306 - Tocopherols (Natural Vitamin E)

### Basic Identification
- **E Number**: E306
- **INS Number**: 306
- **CAS Number**: Multiple (59-02-9 for vitamin E general, 1406-18-4 for d-alpha-tocopherol)
- **Common Name**: Tocopherol-Rich Extract, Mixed Tocopherols
- **Scientific Name**: Tocopherols (mixture of alpha, beta, gamma, delta forms)
- **Japanese Name**: トコフェロール
- **Japanese Label Name**: ビタミンE
- **Aliases**: Natural Vitamin E, Mixed tocopherol concentrate

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: antioxidant
- **Sub-class**: vitamin (essential nutrient)
- **Source Type**: natural (extracted from vegetable oils) or synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: phenolic compounds

### Natural Occurrence
- **Found in Nature**: Yes
- **Natural Sources**: Vegetable oils (soybean, sunflower, corn, olive, rapeseed, palm), wheat germ, nuts (almonds), seeds (sunflower), sea buckthorn berries, whole grains
- **Description**: Vitamin E exists in eight forms: four tocopherols (alpha, beta, gamma, delta) and four tocotrienols. E306 is a mixture rich in tocopherols extracted from natural sources.

### Manufacturing
- **Method**: Extraction from vegetable oils
- **Description**: Commonly extracted from the distillate obtained during deodorization of vegetable oils (especially soybean, canola, sunflower, corn, cottonseed). Soybean oil deodorized distillate is the primary source due to low cost. Can also be extracted using supercritical CO2 from wheat germ.

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E306), quantum satis in most categories
- **USA**: GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe)
- **Japan**: Approved (既存添加物)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved (306)
- **Canada**: Approved
- **EFSA Last Evaluated**: 2015
- **Special Notes**: Evaluated together with E307, E308, E309

### Safety Evaluations
- **JECFA ADI**: 0.15-2 mg/kg bw/day (for dl-α-tocopherol)
- **EFSA ADI**: Not specified (Panel concluded available data too limited to establish ADI, but no safety concern at reported uses and levels)
- **EFSA Evaluation Year**: 2015
- **Safety Notes**: EFSA noted that vitamin E is widely consumed via food, is an essential nutrient, and established Upper Levels (ULs) are not exceeded in any population group except children in one survey from one country. FDA, EFSA, and JECFA all recognize tocopherols as safe when used within permitted levels.

### Commonly Found In
- Breakfast cereals ("Vitamin E added")
- Vegetable oils and cooking oils
- Nuts and nut butters
- Margarine and spreads
- Baked goods
- Snack foods
- Infant formula
- Vitamin supplements

### Fun Facts
1. **Essential Nutrient Disguised as Additive**: Unlike most food additives, tocopherols (Vitamin E) are actually essential nutrients that humans must obtain from diet - they're beneficial compounds, not just preservatives!

2. **Nature's Own Antioxidant**: Found naturally in the same oils it's used to protect. When you eat nuts, seeds, or vegetable oils, you're consuming both the fats AND nature's built-in protection system.

3. **Multiple Forms Working Together**: Exists in 8 different forms (4 tocopherols, 4 tocotrienols), each with slightly different antioxidant powers. Mixed tocopherols provide broader protection than single forms.

4. **Extracted from Waste**: Economically extracted from deodorizer distillate - a by-product of vegetable oil refining - turning what was once waste into valuable vitamin E.

5. **Diet Geography**: α-tocopherol dominates European diets (olive/sunflower oils), while γ-tocopherol is most common in American diets (soybean/corn oils) - your vitamin E profile depends on your cooking oil culture!

### Other Uses
- **Cosmetics**: Anti-aging skincare, antioxidant
- **Pharmaceutical**: Vitamin E supplements, capsule stabilizer
- **Animal feed**: Nutritional supplement, antioxidant

## E307 - Alpha-Tocopherol

### Basic Identification
- **E Number**: E307
- **INS Number**: 307
- **CAS Number**: 59-02-9 (d-alpha-tocopherol), 10191-41-0 (dl-alpha-tocopherol, synthetic)
- **Common Name**: Alpha-Tocopherol
- **Scientific Name**: (2R)-2,5,7,8-Tetramethyl-2-[(4R,8R)-4,8,12-trimethyltridecyl]-3,4-dihydrochromen-6-ol
- **Japanese Name**: α-トコフェロール
- **Japanese Label Name**: ビタミンE (α型)
- **Aliases**: Vitamin E, α-Tocopherol, D-alpha-tocopherol (natural), DL-alpha-tocopherol (synthetic)

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: antioxidant
- **Sub-class**: vitamin (essential nutrient)
- **Source Type**: natural (extracted from vegetable oils) or synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: phenolic compound

### Natural Occurrence
- **Found in Nature**: Yes
- **Natural Sources**: Olive oil, sunflower oil, wheat germ, almonds, hazelnuts, sunflower seeds, spinach, broccoli, kiwi
- **Description**: The most biologically active form of Vitamin E. Main dietary source in European diet (from olive and sunflower oils). The natural form (d-alpha) is more bioavailable than synthetic (dl-alpha).

### Manufacturing
- **Method**: Extraction from vegetable oils or chemical synthesis
- **Description**: Natural d-alpha-tocopherol extracted from vegetable oil deodorizer distillate, particularly from sunflower and wheat germ oils. Synthetic dl-alpha-tocopherol produced through chemical synthesis from petrochemical precursors.

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E307), quantum satis in most categories
- **USA**: GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe)
- **Japan**: Approved (既存添加物)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved (307)
- **Canada**: Approved
- **EFSA Last Evaluated**: 2015
- **Special Notes**: Evaluated together with E306, E308, E309

### Safety Evaluations
- **JECFA ADI**: 0.15-2 mg/kg bw/day (for dl-α-tocopherol)
- **EFSA ADI**: Not specified (no safety concern at reported uses and levels)
- **EFSA Evaluation Year**: 2015
- **Safety Notes**: Most bioactive form of vitamin E. EFSA concluded no safety concerns given widespread consumption as essential nutrient.

### Commonly Found In
- Fortified breakfast cereals
- Vitamin supplements (often labeled "Vitamin E")
- Vegetable oils
- Margarine and spreads
- Infant formula
- Baked goods
- Nuts and nut products
- Functional foods and beverages

### Fun Facts
1. **Vitamin E Champion**: The most biologically active form of all eight vitamin E variants - your body preferentially retains and uses alpha-tocopherol over other forms.

2. **Natural vs. Synthetic Mystery**: Natural d-alpha-tocopherol (from plants) is about 50% more bioactive than synthetic dl-alpha-tocopherol (from chemistry), even though they're nearly identical molecules!

3. **Brain Protector**: Alpha-tocopherol is the predominant form of vitamin E in the brain and nervous system, protecting these critical fatty tissues from oxidative damage.

4. **Sunflower Superstar**: Sunflower oil is richest in alpha-tocopherol, making it a prime source for both dietary vitamin E and commercial extraction.

5. **European Essential**: Main vitamin E source in European diets through olive and sunflower oils, making E307 both a traditional dietary component and a food additive.

### Other Uses
- **Cosmetics**: Anti-aging creams, skin protection
- **Pharmaceutical**: Vitamin E supplements (most common form), antioxidant in drug formulations
- **Animal feed**: Nutritional supplement

- **Common Name**: Gamma-Tocopherol
- **Scientific Name**: 7,8-Dimethyltocol
- **Japanese Name**: γ-トコフェロール
- **Japanese Label Name**: ビタミンE (γ型)
- **Aliases**: γ-Tocopherol, 7,8-Dimethyltocol, DL-γ-Tocopherol

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: antioxidant
- **Sub-class**: vitamin (essential nutrient)
- **Source Type**: natural (extracted from vegetable oils) or synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: phenolic compound

### Natural Occurrence
- **Found in Nature**: Yes
- **Natural Sources**: Soybean oil, corn oil, walnuts, pecans, sesame seeds, pumpkin seeds
- **Description**: A form of vitamin E particularly abundant in American diet due to high consumption of soybean and corn oils. Has unique antioxidant properties complementary to alpha-tocopherol.

### Manufacturing
- **Method**: Extraction from vegetable oils
- **Description**: Extracted primarily from soybean and corn oil deodorizer distillates. Gamma-tocopherol is the predominant tocopherol in these oil sources, making extraction economically efficient.
- **Molecular Formula**: C28H48O2
- **Molecular Weight**: 416.7

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E308), quantum satis in most categories
- **USA**: GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe)
- **Japan**: Approved (既存添加物)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved (308)
- **Canada**: Approved
- **EFSA Last Evaluated**: 2015
- **Special Notes**: Evaluated together with E306, E307, E309

### Safety Evaluations
- **JECFA ADI**: Included in group ADI with other tocopherols (0.15-2 mg/kg bw/day)
- **EFSA ADI**: Not specified (no safety concern at reported uses and levels)
- **EFSA Evaluation Year**: 2015
- **Safety Notes**: Demonstrated effects on protecting cells from oxidative stress, mitigating inflammation, and regulating gene expression in vitro.

### Commonly Found In
- Soybean oil and soy-based products
- Corn oil products
- Mixed vegetable oils
- Margarine and spreads
- Salad dressings
- Baked goods
- Snack foods
- Fortified cereals

### Fun Facts
1. **American Vitamin E**: The most common form of vitamin E in the American diet due to widespread use of soybean and corn oils - Americans consume more gamma than alpha tocopherol!

2. **Nitrogen Specialist**: Uniquely effective at trapping reactive nitrogen species (like peroxynitrite), not just oxygen radicals - offers protection that alpha-tocopherol can't provide.

3. **Soybean Treasure**: Soybean oil is naturally high in gamma-tocopherol, and the deodorizer distillate from soy processing is a major commercial source.

4. **Inflammation Fighter**: Research suggests gamma-tocopherol may have superior anti-inflammatory properties compared to alpha-tocopherol, with unique benefits for cardiovascular health.

5. **Different Antioxidant Strategy**: While alpha-tocopherol is better at integrating into cell membranes, gamma-tocopherol excels at scavenging specific types of free radicals, making them complementary partners.

### Other Uses
- **Pharmaceutical**: Vitamin E supplements (often in mixed tocopherol formulations)
- **Cosmetics**: Antioxidant in skincare
- **Research**: Anti-inflammatory and anti-cancer research

### Other Uses

## E310 - Propyl Gallate

### Basic Identification
- **E Number**: E310
- **INS Number**: 310
- **CAS Number**: 121-79-9
- **Common Name**: Propyl Gallate
- **Scientific Name**: Propyl 3,4,5-trihydroxybenzoate
- **Japanese Name**: 没食子酸プロピル (mokkushokushisan puropyiru)
- **Japanese Label Name**: 没食子酸プロピル
- **Aliases**: Gallic acid propyl ester, n-Propyl gallate

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: antioxidant
- **Sub-class**: synthetic phenolic antioxidant
- **Source Type**: synthetic
- **Chemical Group**: phenolic ester (gallate)

### Natural Occurrence
- **Found in Nature**: No (synthetic ester, though gallic acid occurs naturally in tea, oak bark, witch hazel)
- **Relationship to Nature**: Derived from gallic acid (which occurs naturally) esterified with propanol
- **Description**: Synthetic antioxidant created by esterifying gallic acid with propyl alcohol. Used in food industry since 1948.

### Manufacturing
- **Method**: Chemical synthesis (esterification)
- **Description**: Produced by esterifying gallic acid (3,4,5-trihydroxybenzoic acid) with propanol (propyl alcohol). The resulting compound is a white to creamy-white crystalline powder.
- **Linear Formula**: 3,4,5-(HO)₃C₆H₂CO₂CH₂CH₂CH₃
- **Molecular Weight**: 212.20

### Regulatory Status
- **EU**: Approved (E310) with restrictions - limited food categories, specific maximum levels
- **USA**: GRAS with limits (<0.02% of fat/oil content)
- **Japan**: Approved (指定添加物 #390)
- **Australia/NZ**: Approved (310)
- **Canada**: Approved with restrictions
- **EFSA Last Evaluated**: 2014
- **Special Notes**: E311 (octyl gallate) and E312 (dodecyl gallate) were removed from EU approved list in 2018, but E310 remains approved

### Safety Evaluations
- **JECFA ADI**: 0-1.4 mg/kg bw/day (established 1996, only for propyl gallate among the three gallates)
- **JECFA Evaluation Year**: 1996
- **EFSA ADI**: 0.5 mg/kg bw/day (2014 re-evaluation)
- **EFSA Evaluation Year**: 2014
- **Safety Notes**: SCF (Scientific Committee on Food) in 1976 established group ADI of 0-0.2 mg/kg bw for three gallates. JECFA later allocated ADI only to propyl gallate and did not establish ADIs for octyl and dodecyl gallate.

### Regulatory Restrictions
- **EU**: Only authorized in limited list of food items with maximum quantities defined by regulation for each category
- **USA**: FDA allows <0.02% of fat/oil content (individually or combined with octyl/dodecyl gallate, BHT, BHA, ascorbyl palmitate)
- **Japan**: Used particularly in butter and oil products with defined limits
- **Special Note**: Stronger antioxidative properties than BHT/BHA but relatively insoluble in oil; can cause purple discoloration in some foods

### Commonly Found In
- Vegetable oils and fats
- Margarine (stabilizer)
- Lard and shortening
- Chewing gum
- Dried meats and meat products
- Mayonnaise and emulsions
- Baked goods and snack foods
- Candy
- Dried milk products

### Fun Facts
1. **Vintage Antioxidant**: One of the longest-established synthetic antioxidants in food use, protecting our fats and oils since 1948 - over 75 years of service!

2. **Synergy Superstar**: Works synergistically when combined with citric acid, BHA, or BHT, creating antioxidant combinations more powerful than any single ingredient alone.

3. **Free Radical Terminator**: Donates hydrogen atoms or electrons to neutralize free radicals before they can attack fatty acid chains and trigger the oxidation cascade - stopping rancidity at its source.

4. **The Survivor**: While its cousins octyl gallate (E311) and dodecyl gallate (E312) were removed from EU's approved list in 2018 due to safety concerns, propyl gallate continues to be approved and used.

5. **Purple Problem**: Despite its effectiveness, propyl gallate has a quirk - it can sometimes cause purple discoloration in certain foods, and is relatively insoluble in oil, limiting its applications.

6. **Gallic Acid Origins**: Derived from gallic acid, a compound naturally found in tea leaves, oak bark, and witch hazel - the synthetic propyl ester extends the natural compound's usefulness.

### Other Uses
- **Cosmetics**: Antioxidant preservative in cosmetic formulations
- **Industrial**: Lubricants, biodiesel, adhesives
- **Pharmaceutical**: Antioxidant in drug formulations

## Key Themes and Insights

### Vitamin-Based Antioxidants (E301, E302, E304, E306-E309)
These additives are derived from or are forms of essential vitamins (C and E), serving dual roles as both preservatives and nutrients. They represent the "beneficial" category of food additives - compounds that would be consumed anyway through natural foods.

### Ascorbate Derivatives (E301, E302, E304)
- All derived from Vitamin C (E300)
- E301 and E302 are water-soluble salts (sodium and calcium)
- E304 is fat-soluble ester (with palmitic acid)
- All share "no ADI necessary" status due to vitamin nature
- Particularly important in meat products for color preservation and nitrosamine reduction

### Tocopherols (E306-E309)
- Natural vitamin E compounds, essential nutrients
- E306 is mixed tocopherols (all forms together)
- E307, E308, E309 are individual forms (alpha, gamma, delta)
- Extracted from vegetable oils (often from processing waste/by-products)
- Provide both antioxidant protection and nutritional benefit
- Diet geography matters: Europe gets more alpha (olive/sunflower), America gets more gamma (soy/corn)

### Gallates (E310-E312)
- Synthetic antioxidants with concerning regulatory trajectory
- E311 and E312 removed from EU approval in 2018
- Only E310 (propyl gallate) remains approved, with restrictions
- Longer alkyl chains = higher fat solubility but also higher safety concerns
- JECFA refused to establish ADIs for E311 and E312 - significant red flag
- Demonstrates precautionary principle and dynamic nature of food additive regulation

### Manufacturing Insights
- **Ascorbates**: Simple salt formation or esterification from ascorbic acid
- **Tocopherols**: Extracted from vegetable oil deodorizer distillate (waste → value)
- **Gallates**: Esterification of natural gallic acid with alcohols of varying chain lengths

### Safety Philosophy
The contrast between vitamin-based antioxidants (no ADI necessary, recognized as beneficial nutrients) and synthetic gallates (restrictive ADIs, two removed from approval) illustrates regulatory preference for nature-identical or naturally occurring compounds over purely synthetic alternatives.
