# Research Data: E421-E436 Polyols and Emulsifiers

## E421 - Mannitol

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E421
- **INS Number**: 421
- **CAS Number**: 69-65-8
- **Common Name**: Mannitol
- **Scientific Name**: D-Mannitol
- **Japanese Name**: マンニトール (Mannitōru)
- **Aliases**: Manna sugar, Mannite

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: sweetener, humectant, bulking agent
- **Sub-class**: polyol (sugar alcohol)
- **Source Type**: natural/synthetic (originally from manna ash tree, now mostly synthetic from fructose)
- **Chemical Group**: polyol

### Natural Occurrence
- Found naturally in mushrooms, seaweed, celery, olives, and manna ash tree
- Especially abundant in mushrooms (shiitake, button mushrooms)

### Manufacturing
- Hydrogenation of fructose or mannose
- Can be extracted from seaweed

### Common Foods
- Sugar-free chewing gum
- Sugar-free candies and chocolate
- Dietetic foods
- Pharmaceuticals (coating for tablets)
- Breath mints

### Safety & Regulation
- **ADI**: Not specified (JECFA) - considered safe
- **EU**: Approved (E421), quantum satis in most foods
- **USA**: GRAS status
- **Japan**: Approved as既存添加物
- **Laxative effect**: Can cause digestive issues at high doses (>20g/day)

### Fun Facts
- Named after "manna" from the Bible (sweet secretion from ash trees)
- Has a cooling effect on the tongue (like mint)
- About 50-60% as sweet as sugar
- Used in medical diagnostics (kidney function tests)
- Less hygroscopic than other polyols (doesn't absorb water easily)

## E422 - Glycerol (Glycerin)

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E422
- **INS Number**: 422
- **CAS Number**: 56-81-5
- **Common Name**: Glycerol, Glycerin, Glycerine
- **Scientific Name**: 1,2,3-Propanetriol
- **Japanese Name**: グリセリン (Guriserin)
- **Aliases**: Glycyl alcohol, Propanetriol

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: humectant, sweetener, solvent, preservative
- **Sub-class**: polyol (trihydric alcohol)
- **Source Type**: natural/synthetic (vegetable oils, animal fats, or petrochemical)
- **Chemical Group**: polyol, alcohol

### Natural Occurrence
- Backbone of all fats and oils (triglycerides)
- Found in all living cells
- Released naturally during soap-making (saponification)

### Manufacturing
- **Vegetable source**: Byproduct of biodiesel and soap production from vegetable oils (soy, palm, coconut)
- **Synthetic**: From propylene (petrochemical origin)
- **Animal source**: From animal fats (less common now)

### Common Foods & Uses
**Food**:
- Baked goods (moisture retention)
- Sugar-free gum and candies
- Frosting and icing
- Low-fat foods
- Beverages

**Non-food** (ubiquitous):
- Cosmetics and skincare (moisturizer)
- Toothpaste and mouthwash
- Pharmaceuticals (suppositories, cough syrups)
- Antifreeze and de-icing fluids
- Explosives (nitroglycerin)
- E-cigarette liquid

### Safety & Regulation
- **ADI**: Not specified - considered safe with no upper limit
- **EU**: Approved (E422), quantum satis
- **USA**: GRAS status
- **Japan**: Approved
- **Generally recognized as one of the safest additives**

### Fun Facts
- One of the most versatile substances on Earth - used in food, cosmetics, medicine, and explosives
- Nitroglycerin (explosive and heart medicine) is made from glycerol
- Sweet taste (about 60% as sweet as sugar)
- Discovered in 1779 during soap-making
- Can be made into dynamite (Alfred Nobel's invention)
- Vegetarian glycerin vs animal glycerin - most modern glycerin is vegan

## E425 - Konjac

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E425
- **INS Number**: 425
- **CAS Number**: 37220-17-0 (glucomannan)
- **Common Name**: Konjac (i: gum, ii: glucomannan)
- **Scientific Name**: Amorphophallus konjac (plant), Glucomannan (polysaccharide)
- **Japanese Name**: コンニャク (Konnyaku) / グルコマンナン
- **Aliases**: Konjac gum, Konjac flour, Glucomannan, Devil's tongue, Voodoo lily

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: thickener, gelling agent, emulsifier
- **Sub-class**: natural polysaccharide
- **Source Type**: plant-derived (konjac root/corm)
- **Chemical Group**: polysaccharide (water-soluble dietary fiber)

### Natural Occurrence
- From the corm (bulb) of the konjac plant (Amorphophallus konjac)
- Native to Southeast Asia (China, Japan, Korea, Indonesia)
- Traditional Japanese food for over 1,000 years

### Manufacturing
- Konjac corms are harvested, dried, and ground into flour
- Glucomannan is extracted and purified
- Needs alkali (calcium hydroxide) to form gel

### Common Foods
- Shirataki noodles (konjac noodles - zero calorie)
- Konnyaku jelly (Japanese traditional food)
- Vegan jelly and gummy candies
- Weight-loss supplements (fiber supplement)
- Low-calorie rice substitute
- Asian desserts

### Safety & Regulation
- **ADI**: Not specified (JECFA) - considered safe
- **EU**: Approved (E425)
- **USA**: GRAS as dietary fiber; FDA warning for konjac candy (choking hazard)
- **Japan**: Traditional food for centuries
- **Choking risk**: Gel forms can cause choking, especially in children and elderly (banned in some forms in EU/USA)

### Fun Facts
- Called "Devil's tongue" because the plant's flower looks like a giant purple tongue
- The konjac plant has one of the largest flowers in the world and smells like rotting flesh
- Shirataki noodles are called "miracle noodles" (zero calories, zero carbs)
- 97% water when made into noodles
- Cannot be digested by humans - pure soluble fiber
- Traditional Japanese New Year food (osechi ryori)

## E426 - Soybean Hemicellulose

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E426
- **INS Number**: 426
- **CAS Number**: 9034-32-6
- **Common Name**: Soybean hemicellulose
- **Scientific Name**: Soybean polysaccharide
- **Japanese Name**: 大豆多糖類 (Daizu Tōrui)
- **Aliases**: Soybean polysaccharide, Soy fiber, Soy soluble polysaccharide

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: thickener, stabiliser, emulsifier
- **Sub-class**: natural polysaccharide
- **Source Type**: plant-derived (soybean)
- **Chemical Group**: polysaccharide (complex carbohydrate)

### Natural Occurrence
- From soybean cell walls (byproduct of soy processing)
- Component of dietary fiber in soybeans

### Manufacturing
- Extracted from soybean cotyledon fiber (okara - tofu production waste)
- Water extraction and purification
- Sustainable use of food processing byproduct

### Common Foods
- Beverages (protein drinks, smoothies)
- Dressings and sauces
- Dairy alternatives
- Bakery products
- Processed meats (stabilizer)

### Safety & Regulation
- **ADI**: Not specified - considered safe
- **EU**: Approved (E426)
- **USA**: GRAS
- **Japan**: Approved (既存添加物)
- **Allergen note**: May contain soy allergen traces

### Fun Facts
- Made from tofu production waste (okara) - sustainable additive
- Excellent emulsifier for oil-in-water emulsions
- Stable over wide pH and temperature range
- Helps reduce fat separation in products

## E427 - Cassia Gum

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E427
- **INS Number**: 427
- **CAS Number**: 9000-76-4
- **Common Name**: Cassia gum
- **Scientific Name**: Cassia tora galactomannan, Cassia occidentalis polysaccharide
- **Japanese Name**: カシアガム (Kashia Gamu)
- **Aliases**: Cassia tora gum, Chinese senna

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: thickener, gelling agent
- **Sub-class**: natural polysaccharide (galactomannan)
- **Source Type**: plant-derived (cassia seeds)
- **Chemical Group**: polysaccharide

### Natural Occurrence
- From seeds of Cassia tora or Cassia occidentalis plants
- Plants native to tropical regions of Asia and Americas
- Seeds also used in traditional Chinese medicine

### Manufacturing
- Seeds are dehulled, ground, and extracted
- Endosperm is separated and milled into powder

### Common Foods
- Pet food (gelling agent for canned pet food - main use)
- Cheese and dairy products
- Meat products
- Bakery goods

### Safety & Regulation
- **ADI**: 0-1.5 mg/kg body weight (JECFA)
- **EU**: Approved (E427), restricted use
- **USA**: Approved for pet food, limited food use
- **Japan**: Approved

### Fun Facts
- Primarily used in pet food (canned dog and cat food)
- Less common in human food than other gums
- Similar to locust bean gum (E410) but cheaper
- Seeds are roasted and used as coffee substitute in some cultures

## E428 - Gelatin

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E428
- **INS Number**: 428
- **CAS Number**: 9000-70-8
- **Common Name**: Gelatin, Gelatine
- **Scientific Name**: Hydrolyzed collagen
- **Japanese Name**: ゼラチン (Zerachin)
- **Aliases**: Gel, Animal gelatin, Collagen hydrolysate

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: gelling agent, thickener, stabiliser, emulsifier
- **Sub-class**: animal protein
- **Source Type**: animal-derived (pork, beef, fish, or poultry)
- **Chemical Group**: protein (collagen derivative)

### Natural Occurrence
- Derived from collagen in animal connective tissue, bones, and skin
- Not naturally occurring in this form (must be extracted and processed)

### Manufacturing
- Extracted from animal bones, skin, and connective tissue
- **Type A** (acid-processed): from pork skin (most common)
- **Type B** (alkali-processed): from cattle bones and hide
- **Fish gelatin**: from fish skin and bones
- Hydrolysis breaks down collagen into gelatin

### Common Foods
- Marshmallows and gummy candies (gummy bears, jelly beans)
- Jello and dessert gels
- Yogurt and dairy desserts
- Mousses and panna cotta
- Pharmaceutical capsules (pill coating)
- Frosted cereals and pop-tarts
- Cheesecake and tiramisu

### Safety & Regulation
- **ADI**: Not specified - considered safe
- **EU**: Approved (E428)
- **USA**: GRAS
- **Japan**: Approved (既存添加物)
- **Religious/dietary restrictions**:
  - **Pork gelatin**: Not kosher or halal
  - **Beef gelatin**: Kosher/halal versions available from certified sources
  - **Fish gelatin**: Kosher (pareve), suitable for pescatarians
  - **Not vegetarian/vegan** (animal product)

### Fun Facts
- Made from animal collagen (skin, bones, connective tissue)
- Pork gelatin is most common (60% of gelatin), beef 30%, fish 10%
- Kosher and halal gelatin exists: from kosher-slaughtered cattle or fish
- Jews debate whether fish gelatin is pareve (neutral) or fleishig (meat)
- Vegetarian alternatives: agar-agar (E406), pectin (E440), carrageenan (E407)
- Melts at body temperature (37°C) - that's why jello "melts in your mouth"
- Used in old photography (gelatin silver prints)
- Can hold up to 10 times its weight in water

## E431 - Polyoxyethylene (40) Stearate

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E431
- **INS Number**: 431
- **CAS Number**: 9004-99-3
- **Common Name**: Polyoxyl 40 stearate
- **Scientific Name**: Polyoxyethylene (40) stearate, PEG-40 stearate
- **Japanese Name**: ポリオキシエチレン(40)ステアリン酸 (Poriokishieren 40 Sutearin-san)
- **Aliases**: Polyethylene glycol 40 stearate, Macrogol stearate, PEG-40 stearate

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: emulsifier, surfactant
- **Sub-class**: synthetic
- **Source Type**: synthetic (stearic acid + ethylene oxide)
- **Chemical Group**: polyoxyethylene ester

### Natural Occurrence
- Not found in nature
- Stearic acid component can be from vegetable or animal fats

### Manufacturing
- Reaction of stearic acid with polyethylene glycol (40 units of ethylene oxide)
- Stearic acid from vegetable oils (palm, coconut) or animal fats

### Common Foods
- Baked goods
- Cake mixes
- Whipped toppings
- Ice cream
- Desserts
- More common in pharmaceuticals and cosmetics

### Safety & Regulation
- **ADI**: 0-25 mg/kg body weight (JECFA, for all polyoxyethylene stearates)
- **EU**: Approved (E431)
- **USA**: GRAS
- **Japan**: Approved

### Fun Facts
- The "40" refers to the number of ethylene oxide units
- More commonly used in cosmetics (creams, lotions) than food
- Related to PEG (polyethylene glycol) - laxative ingredient

## E432 - Polysorbate 20 (Tween 20)

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E432
- **INS Number**: 432
- **CAS Number**: 9005-64-5
- **Common Name**: Polysorbate 20, Tween 20
- **Scientific Name**: Polyoxyethylene (20) sorbitan monolaurate
- **Japanese Name**: ポリソルベート20 (Porisōbēto 20)
- **Aliases**: Tween 20, PS20

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: emulsifier, surfactant, dispersing agent
- **Sub-class**: synthetic
- **Source Type**: synthetic (from sorbitol + lauric acid + ethylene oxide)
- **Chemical Group**: polyoxyethylene sorbitan ester

### Natural Occurrence
- Not found in nature
- Lauric acid component from coconut or palm kernel oil

### Manufacturing
- Sorbitan (from sorbitol) + lauric acid → sorbitan monolaurate
- Ethoxylation with ethylene oxide (20 units)

### Common Foods
- Ice cream and frozen desserts
- Baked goods and cake mixes
- Salad dressings
- Pickles
- Vitamins and supplements (helps dissolve fat-soluble vitamins)

### Safety & Regulation
- **ADI**: 0-25 mg/kg body weight (JECFA, for all polysorbates)
- **EU**: Approved (E432)
- **USA**: GRAS, 21 CFR 172.840
- **Japan**: Approved

### Fun Facts
- "Tween" name because it's in-between hydrophilic (water-loving) and lipophilic (fat-loving)
- The "20" refers to the fatty acid: 20 = lauric acid (12 carbons)
- Used in COVID-19 mRNA vaccines (helps stabilize lipid nanoparticles)
- Common in cosmetics and pharmaceuticals

## E433 - Polysorbate 80 (Tween 80)

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E433
- **INS Number**: 433
- **CAS Number**: 9005-65-6
- **Common Name**: Polysorbate 80, Tween 80
- **Scientific Name**: Polyoxyethylene (20) sorbitan monooleate
- **Japanese Name**: ポリソルベート80 (Porisōbēto 80)
- **Aliases**: Tween 80, PS80

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: emulsifier, surfactant, dispersing agent
- **Sub-class**: synthetic
- **Source Type**: synthetic (from sorbitol + oleic acid + ethylene oxide)
- **Chemical Group**: polyoxyethylene sorbitan ester

### Natural Occurrence
- Not found in nature
- Oleic acid component from vegetable oils (olive, soybean, sunflower)

### Manufacturing
- Sorbitan (from sorbitol) + oleic acid → sorbitan monooleate
- Ethoxylation with ethylene oxide (20 units)

### Common Foods
- Ice cream (prevents ice crystal formation)
- Whipped toppings
- Cottage cheese
- Chocolate milk and flavored milk
- Bakery products
- Pickles and pickle products
- Vitamin supplements

### Safety & Regulation
- **ADI**: 0-25 mg/kg body weight (JECFA, for all polysorbates combined)
- **EU**: Approved (E433)
- **USA**: GRAS, 21 CFR 172.840
- **Japan**: Approved

### Fun Facts
- Most widely used polysorbate (more than Tween 20, 40, 60, 65 combined)
- The "80" refers to the fatty acid: 80 = oleic acid (18 carbons, 1 double bond)
- Used in many vaccines and injectable medicines
- Some controversy: rare allergic reactions, potential gut microbiome effects (still being studied)
- Can help drugs cross the blood-brain barrier (used in neurology research)

## E436 - Polysorbate 65 (Tween 65)

### Basic Information
- **E Number**: E436
- **INS Number**: 436
- **CAS Number**: 9005-71-4
- **Common Name**: Polysorbate 65, Tween 65
- **Scientific Name**: Polyoxyethylene (20) sorbitan tristearate
- **Japanese Name**: ポリソルベート65 (Porisōbēto 65)
- **Aliases**: Tween 65, PS65

### Classification
- **Functional Class**: emulsifier, surfactant
- **Sub-class**: synthetic
- **Source Type**: synthetic (from sorbitol + stearic acid + ethylene oxide)
- **Chemical Group**: polyoxyethylene sorbitan ester

### Natural Occurrence
- Not found in nature
- Stearic acid from vegetable oils or animal fats

### Manufacturing
- Sorbitan (from sorbitol) + stearic acid (3 molecules) → sorbitan tristearate
- Ethoxylation with ethylene oxide (20 units)
- Note: "tri-" means 3 stearic acid chains (vs "mono-" for others)

### Common Foods
- Cake mixes and baked goods
- Whipped toppings
- Yeast-raised bakery products
- Coffee whiteners
- Least common of all polysorbates in food

### Safety & Regulation
- **ADI**: 0-25 mg/kg body weight (JECFA, for all polysorbates combined)
- **EU**: Approved (E436)
- **USA**: GRAS, 21 CFR 172.840
- **Japan**: Approved

### Fun Facts
- Only polysorbate with THREE fatty acid chains (tristearate vs monostearate)
- The "65" refers to the fatty acid: 60 = stearic acid, "5" indicates tri-ester form
- Least commonly used polysorbate in food industry
- More lipophilic (fat-loving) than other Tweens due to three fatty acid chains
- Creates more stable emulsions in high-fat products

## Religious & Dietary Considerations

### E428 Gelatin
- **HARAM/NOT KOSHER**: Pork gelatin (most common - 60% of market)
- **HALAL/KOSHER**: Beef gelatin from certified slaughter
- **KOSHER (Pareve)**: Fish gelatin
- **NOT VEGAN/VEGETARIAN**: All gelatin (animal-derived)
- **Alternatives**: Agar (E406), Pectin (E440), Carrageenan (E407)

### E422 Glycerol
- **Usually VEGAN**: Modern glycerol mostly from vegetable oils (biodiesel byproduct)
- **Check source**: Can be from animal fats (rare) or petrochemical
- **Look for**: "Vegetable glycerin" on label

### E426 Soybean Hemicellulose
- **Allergen**: Contains soy - avoid if soy allergy

### Polysorbates (E432-436)
- **Usually VEGAN**: Fatty acids typically from vegetable oils
- **Verify source**: Stearic/palmitic acid can be animal-derived (rarely)

## Key Highlights for JSON Creation

1. **E422 Glycerol** - Most ubiquitous additive on this list (food, cosmetics, explosives, antifreeze)
2. **E428 Gelatin** - Only animal-derived additive, significant religious restrictions
3. **E425 Konjac** - Traditional Japanese food, "zero calorie" noodles, choking hazard warning
4. **E433 Polysorbate 80** - Most widely used emulsifier, in COVID vaccines, some controversy
5. **Polysorbate naming** - "Tween" because between hydrophilic and lipophilic
6. **ADI groupings**: All polysorbates share one group ADI (0-25 mg/kg combined)
