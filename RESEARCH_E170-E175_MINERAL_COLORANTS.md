## E170 - Calcium Carbonate

### Basic Information
- **CAS Number:** 471-34-1
- **INS Number:** 170
- **Scientific Name:** Calcium carbonate
- **Japanese Name:** 炭酸カルシウム (Tansan karusiumumu)
- **Aliases:** Chalk, Limestone, Calcite, Aragonite

### Functional Class
**Multi-functional additive:**
- Colour (white)
- Acidity regulator
- Anticaking agent
- Calcium source for fortification

### Source Type
**Mineral** - Extracted from limestone, marble, or chalk deposits

### Regulatory Status
- **Globally Approved:** EU, USA (GRAS), Japan, Australia/NZ, Canada
- **EU Status:** Approved, quantum satis
- **USA Status:** GRAS (21 CFR 184.1191)
- **Restrictions:** None - widely approved for general use

### ADI (Acceptable Daily Intake)
- **JECFA ADI:** Not specified (considered safe at current use levels)
- **EFSA ADI:** Not specified (no safety concerns)

### Commonly Found In
- Flour and bread (calcium fortification)
- Chewing gum
- Antacid tablets (Tums, Rolaids)
- Calcium-fortified orange juice
- Baking powder
- Breakfast cereals
- Confectionery

### Manufacturing
Extracted from natural limestone or marble deposits through quarrying, grinding, and purification. Can also be produced synthetically through precipitation from calcium hydroxide and CO2.

### Fun Facts
- **You're eating chalk!** E170 is literally the same material as blackboard chalk
- Most abundant mineral compound on Earth
- Dual function as both colorant AND calcium supplement
- Forms the shells of marine organisms and coral reefs
- Used by Romans in construction 2000+ years ago

## E172 - Iron Oxides and Hydroxides

### Basic Information
- **CAS Numbers:** 1309-37-1, 1332-37-2, 51274-00-1
- **INS Number:** 172
- **Scientific Name:** Iron oxides and iron hydroxides
- **Japanese Name:** 酸化鉄 (Sankatetsu)
- **Aliases:** Iron Oxide Red (CI 77491), Iron Oxide Yellow (CI 77492), Iron Oxide Black (CI 77499), Rust, Ferric Oxide

### Functional Class
**Colour only** - Available in red, yellow, black, and brown shades

### Source Type
**Mineral/Metallic** - From iron ore deposits or synthetic production

### Regulatory Status
- **Widely Approved:** EU, USA, Japan, Australia/NZ, Canada
- **EU Status:** Approved, quantum satis
- **USA Status:** Exempt from certification (21 CFR 73.200)
- **Restrictions:** Not to exceed 0.1% by weight in sausage casings (USA)

### ADI (Acceptable Daily Intake)
- **JECFA ADI:** Not specified (no safety concerns at current use levels)
- **EFSA ADI:** Not specified (acceptable at current use levels)

### Commonly Found In
- **Mars bars** (iconic reddish-brown color)
- Salmon paste and fish spreads
- Sausage casings
- Confectionery and candy coatings
- Cake decorations
- Bakery products
- Nutritional supplements (as iron source)

### Manufacturing
Food-grade iron oxides are typically produced synthetically through controlled oxidation of iron salts or by calcination of iron compounds. The different colors depend on the oxidation state and hydration of iron.

### Fun Facts
- **You're eating actual rust!** Iron oxides are chemically identical to rust
- Different colors from the same metal: red, yellow, black, brown
- One of the safest colorants available
- Used in ancient cave paintings 40,000+ years ago
- Can also serve as an iron supplement
- The color of Mars (the Red Planet) comes from iron oxide

## E173 - Aluminium

### Basic Information
- **CAS Number:** 7429-90-5
- **INS Number:** 173
- **Scientific Name:** Aluminium (metallic)
- **Japanese Name:** アルミニウム (Aruminiumu)
- **Aliases:** Aluminum, CI 77000, Aluminium powder, Metallic aluminium

### Functional Class
**Surface colorant only** - For external decoration only, not internal use

### Source Type
**Metallic** - Pure metallic aluminium

### Regulatory Status
- **EU:** Approved (external decoration only)
- **USA:** **BANNED** - Not approved for food use
- **Japan:** Approved (external surface decoration only)
- **Australia/NZ:** **BANNED** - Not permitted
- **Canada:** Approved (surface decoration only)

### ADI (Acceptable Daily Intake)
- **JECFA ADI:** Not specified (limited oral absorption; use restricted to surface only)
- **EFSA ADI:** Not specified (minimal exposure due to external use only)

### Commonly Found In
**EU and approved markets only:**
- Cake decorations
- Confectionery external coatings
- Dragées and sugar-coated almonds
- Decorative edible pearls

### Manufacturing
Produced through the Hall-Héroult process: extracting aluminium from bauxite ore through electrolysis. The metal is then ground into fine powder or flakes for use as a surface colorant.

### Fun Facts
- **Banned in USA despite EU approval!** One of the few additives with this distinction
- You're eating actual metal (but only the outside coating)
- Most abundant metal in Earth's crust
- Never occurs naturally in pure metallic form
- EU allows it, but only for external decoration - you're not supposed to eat it directly
- Also banned in Australia

## E174 - Silver

### Basic Information
- **CAS Number:** 7440-22-4
- **INS Number:** 174
- **Scientific Name:** Silver (metallic)
- **Japanese Name:** 銀 (Gin)
- **Aliases:** Argentum, CI 77820, Edible silver, Silver leaf, Vark/Varak (Indian silver leaf)

### Functional Class
**Surface colorant** - Decorative use only

### Source Type
**Metallic** - Precious metal (99.9%+ pure)

### Regulatory Status
- **Globally Approved BUT Highly Restricted:**
- **EU:** Approved (external decoration only, quantum satis)
- **USA:** Approved (GRAS for decorative purposes only, 21 CFR 189.250)
- **Japan:** Approved (decorative, traditional use in confectionery)
- **Australia/NZ:** Approved (decorative purposes only)
- **Canada:** Approved (surface decoration only)

### ADI (Acceptable Daily Intake)
- **JECFA ADI:** Not specified (very limited absorption; decorative use only)
- **EFSA ADI:** Not specified (negligible exposure)

### Commonly Found In
**Luxury/decorative applications only:**
- Luxury chocolates and pralines (silver leaf)
- High-end wedding cakes (edible silver decoration)
- Indian sweets - Barfi, Peda (varak silver leaf)
- Premium liqueurs (silver flakes)
- Gourmet desserts at fine dining restaurants
- Decorative dragées and Jordan almonds

### Manufacturing
Produced by refining silver from ores or recycled sources, then hammered into extremely thin sheets (silver leaf) or ground into fine powder. Food-grade silver must be at least 99.9% pure.

### Fun Facts
- **You're literally eating silver!** Pure precious metal on your food
- Traditional in Indian cuisine (varak) for centuries
- Chemically inert - passes through digestive system unchanged
- Silver leaf can be hammered so thin it's nearly transparent
- Approved globally but ONLY for decoration - you're eating precious metal for aesthetics only!
- Has antimicrobial properties (used in medical dressings)

## E175 - Gold

### Basic Information
- **CAS Number:** 7440-57-5
- **INS Number:** 175
- **Scientific Name:** Gold (metallic)
- **Japanese Name:** 金 (Kin)
- **Aliases:** Aurum, CI 77480, Edible gold, Gold leaf, 24-karat gold leaf

### Functional Class
**Surface colorant** - Decorative use only

### Source Type
**Metallic** - Precious metal (22-24 karat purity)

### Regulatory Status
- **Globally Approved BUT Highly Restricted:**
- **EU:** Approved (external decoration only, quantum satis)
- **USA:** Approved (GRAS for decorative purposes only, 21 CFR 189.100)
- **Japan:** Approved (decorative, traditional use in sake and confectionery)
- **Australia/NZ:** Approved (decorative purposes only)
- **Canada:** Approved (surface decoration only)

### ADI (Acceptable Daily Intake)
- **JECFA ADI:** Not specified (gold is chemically inert; virtually no absorption)
- **EFSA ADI:** Not specified (negligible exposure)

### Commonly Found In
**Ultra-luxury/decorative applications:**
- Ultra-luxury chocolates and truffles (24k gold leaf)
- High-end wedding and celebration cakes
- Premium liqueurs (Goldschläger, Danziger Goldwasser with gold flakes)
- Gourmet desserts at Michelin-starred restaurants
- Luxury ice cream and gelato
- Premium sushi and Japanese cuisine garnish
- Expensive coffee drinks and cocktails

### Manufacturing
Produced by refining gold from ores or recycled sources, then hammered into extremely thin sheets (typically 0.1-0.2 micrometers thick) or ground into fine powder or flakes. Food-grade gold must be at least 90% pure (22 karat), preferably 99.9% (24 karat).

### Fun Facts
- **You're literally eating gold!** 24-karat precious metal on your dessert
- The ultimate luxury food decoration
- Completely inert - your body can't absorb or digest it at all
- Gold leaf is hammered so thin you can see light through it
- A single gram of gold can be hammered into a sheet covering 1 square meter
- Traditional in Japanese sake (kinpaku sake) and Indian sweets
- Passes through digestive system completely unchanged - you excrete gold!
- One of the most expensive food additives by weight, but used in microscopic amounts

## Key Patterns & Insights

### Regulatory Divergence
- **E170 & E172:** Universally approved with minimal restrictions
- **E173:** Banned in USA/Australia despite EU approval
- **E174 & E175:** Approved globally but VERY restricted (decorative only)

### Absorption & Safety
- **E170:** Actively absorbed (calcium source)
- **E172:** Minimal absorption
- **E173/E174/E175:** Virtually no absorption (chemically inert metals)

### ADI Philosophy
- **E170 & E172:** Have established safety data but ADI "not specified" due to low toxicity
- **E173/E174/E175:** ADI "not specified" because absorption is negligible/zero

### Usage Hierarchy
1. **E170:** Functional (color + calcium + anticaking)
2. **E172:** Color only (multiple shades)
3. **E173:** Surface decoration (external only)
4. **E174/E175:** Luxury decoration (precious metals, aesthetics only)

### "You're Eating Metal" Spectrum
- **E170:** Eating chalk (mineral carbonate)
- **E172:** Eating rust (metal oxide)
- **E173:** Eating aluminium (metal, surface only)
- **E174:** Eating silver (precious metal, decorative only)
- **E175:** Eating gold (precious metal, decorative only)
